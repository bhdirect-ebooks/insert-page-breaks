Paul and the Corinthian Church

e Corinth that Paul visits was one lled with sanctuaries, statues, shops,

an agora, and a forum with tribunal that probably inspired his own de-

piction of Christ on the judgment seat (2 Cor 5:10). A number of small

shop keepers, artisans, teachers, and secretaries were among the people of

Corinth whom Appian considers poor (Hist. 8.136).

36

Alciphron speaks of

the area having both immorally rich and miserably poor (Ep. 3.24[iii.60]).

Dio Chrysostom, writing in the rst century CE, describes the rabble at

the Isthmian games: “One could hear crowds of wretched sophists around

Poseidon’s temple shouting and reviling one another, and their disciples . . .

ghting with one another, many writers reading aloud their stupid works,

many poets reciting their poems while others applauded them, many jug-

glers showing their tricks, many fortune-tellers interpreting fortunes, law-

yers innumerable perverting judgment, and peddlers not a few peddling

whatever they happened to have” (Or. 8.9).
